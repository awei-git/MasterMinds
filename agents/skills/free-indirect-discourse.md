# Free Indirect Discourse (自由間接話語)

## One-liner
Blend third-person narration with a character's inner voice — no attribution tags, no person-shift, just the character's diction colonizing the narrator's sentences.

## The Problem
Third-person narration has three modes for rendering thought, and the first two fail:
- Direct thought: *She thought, I hate this.* — The attribution tag breaks immersion.
- Reported thought: *She thought that she hated this.* — The subordinate clause kills urgency.
- Free indirect: *God, she hated this.* — The character's voice, the narrator's grammar. No tag. No distance.

Free indirect gives you intimacy without surrendering narrative control.

## Revision Moves

Eight operations that convert tagged thought into free indirect.

**1. Delete the attribution tag.**
- Before: *She thought that the house looked wrong.*
- After: *The house looked wrong.*
- If the POV is established, the reader knows whose perception this is.

**2. Shift from subordinate clause to independent sentence.**
- Before: *He realized that nobody was coming.*
- After: *Nobody was coming.*
- Subordination ("he realized that…") creates syntactic distance. An independent sentence has the force of direct experience.

**3. Swap narrator vocabulary for character vocabulary.**
- Before: *The terms of the offer were unfavorable.*
- After: *The offer was a joke. A sick joke.*
- The character's register, emphasis, and slang must replace the narrator's neutral diction. Test: could this sentence come from any character in the book, or only this one? If any character, the swap hasn't happened.
- **Zoom application:** Control distance by how much character vocabulary you inject. *"The city was quiet"* (narrator). *"The city felt too quiet"* (filtered). *"God, this silence"* (character). At close range, vocabulary, sentence length, and emotional temperature all belong to the character.

**4. Convert declarations into unattributed questions.**
- Before: *She wondered whether he was serious.*
- After: *Was he serious?*
- Unattributed questions in third-person narration are almost always the character thinking. The question IS consciousness.

**5. Add fragments and interrupted rhythm.**
- Before: *He looked at the empty apartment and felt the full weight of what he had lost.*
- After: *The empty apartment. The marks on the wall where the frames had been. Christ.*
- Fragment sentences mimic associative thought. Complete sentences signal narration; fragments signal interiority.

**6. Hold third person throughout.**
- Before: *I can't believe this, she thought.*
- After: *She couldn't believe this.*
- The character's voice takes over diction, rhythm, temperature — but the grammar stays "she/he," never "I." That constraint is what makes it free indirect rather than interior monologue.

**7. Create ironic distance through contradiction.**
- Before: *She wanted to seem calm but was drinking too much.*
- After: *She would handle this with grace and dignity. She poured herself a fourth glass of wine.*
- The first sentence is the character's voice. The second is the narrator's correction. The gap is irony — no commentary needed. Use when a character is self-deceiving: let their free indirect self-narration run, then contradict it with a single observed detail.

**8. Use temporal markers to reveal character.**
- Before: *She kept putting things off, believing they would improve.*
- After: *Tomorrow she would fix everything. Tomorrow was always the day things got fixed.*
- Repetition and resignation belong to the character. The narrator holds steady while the character reveals herself through her own habitual phrasing.

## Entry and Exit Patterns

The moves above convert individual sentences. These four patterns handle the harder problem: how to cross the border between narrator and character within a paragraph without a visible seam.

**Sensory anchor entry.** Start with a physical perception, then let the character's interpretation take over.
> *The kitchen smelled like burnt coffee.* [narrator reports sense data] *Three days of it now, coating everything, that acrid film on the counters.* [character's annoyance has colonized the sentence — "acrid film" is her vocabulary, not reportage]

The sense detail is the hinge. The reader accepts the narrator's observation, then slides into the character's reaction without noticing the shift.

**Perception verb fade.** Use one light perception verb at the threshold, then drop all verbs of cognition.
> *She noticed the gap on the shelf where his books had been.* [filtered — "noticed" still present] *A clean rectangle in the dust. He'd been thorough.* [free indirect — no attribution, character's bitter tone]

One "noticed" or "saw" orients the reader. After that, delete every subsequent perception verb — the POV is established.

**Detail escalation exit.** Return to narrator control by pulling back to a detail the character wouldn't self-report.
> *The salary was an insult. The non-compete was a cage.* [free indirect — character's voice] *She folded the contract along its original creases and placed it on the table.* [narrator — precise physical action the character wouldn't narrate about herself]

Physical, observed, uninflected actions signal the narrator resuming control. The character's voice doesn't describe her own hands.

**Hard cut exit.** End the free indirect passage with a fragment or question, then jump to a new paragraph of pure narration.
> *Was he serious? Was any of this serious?*
>
> *Outside, the parking lot was emptying.* [new paragraph, narrator voice, no character diction]

The white space does the work. A paragraph break after a character's unattributed question is a clean exit.

## Transformation in Practice

The same moment, three ways:

**Direct thought (tagged):**
> Sarah looked at the contract. *This is insane*, she thought. *They want me to sign away three years for this?* She felt a surge of anger. *Do they think I'm stupid?*

**Reported thought (subordinated):**
> Sarah looked at the contract. She thought it was insane and felt that signing away three years for such terms was unacceptable. She wondered if they thought she was stupid.

**Free indirect discourse:**
> Sarah looked at the contract. [**Sensory anchor entry** — physical action is the hinge] Three years. [**Move 2** — promoted from "she realized that it was three years"] They wanted three years for this. [**Move 1** — "she thought" deleted] The salary was an insult, the non-compete was a cage, and the equity — she almost laughed — the equity was a rounding error. [**Move 3** — *insult*, *cage*, *rounding error* are Sarah's vocabulary, not the narrator's] Did they think she couldn't read? [**Move 4** — unattributed question replaces "she wondered"]

Note what's absent: no first-person break (**Move 6** held), no "she felt angry" (**Move 5** — fragments and escalation produce the anger instead).

## Diagnostic Triggers

Search your draft for these specific patterns. Each one is a free indirect opportunity.

| Find this | It means | Apply |
|---|---|---|
| "she thought" / "he wondered" / "she realized" more than once per page | Attribution tags are doing work the POV could handle | Moves 1–2: delete tag, promote to independent sentence |
| A paragraph of action followed by a paragraph starting with a thought verb | Seam between exterior and interior | Sensory anchor entry or perception verb fade: infect the action paragraph with character vocabulary so interiority bleeds in before the "thought" paragraph |
| Two POV characters whose narration reads identically | Your voice is overriding theirs | Move 3: list five words only this character would use, then rewrite the passage using at least three |
| A character making a claim the plot contradicts | Self-deception on the page | Move 7: let their free indirect run, follow with one contradicting detail |
| "She felt [emotion]" / "He was filled with [emotion]" | Telling emotion instead of rendering it | Move 5: replace the emotion label with the fragments and sensory details that produce that emotion in the reader |
| A question in dialogue that's really internal | The character is thinking out loud for the reader's benefit | Move 4: cut it from dialogue, place it as an unattributed question in narration |
| First person ("I") appearing mid-scene outside dialogue | POV slipped from free indirect into interior monologue | Move 6: convert to third person, keep the character's diction intact |
| "Tomorrow" / "someday" / "eventually" / "one day" repeated near self-promises | Character locked in a habitual delay loop | Move 8: let the temporal marker repeat — the repetition itself is the characterization |
| Free indirect passage ending with "She turned back to…" or similar weak connector | Exit seam is visible | Detail escalation exit or hard cut exit: replace the connector with a precise physical action or a paragraph break |

## Pitfall Tests

**Indistinguishable voices** — *Test:* Cover the character's name. Can you tell whose head you're in from diction alone? Swap two characters' free indirect passages — if nothing feels wrong, you're writing in your own voice. *Fix:* Before drafting a scene, write three sentences of raw interior monologue for that character — their words, their grammar. Use that as a key for Move 3. Each POV character needs specific vocabulary, sentence-length tendencies, and recurring fixations.

**Overuse** — *Test:* Read a page aloud. If you can't tell where the narrator stands apart from the character, you've lost narrative authority. *Fix:* Reserve free indirect for emotional pressure points. Exposition, scene-setting, and temporal jumps need clear narrator voice. Use the exit patterns to return to narration after each pressure point.

**Jarring transitions** — *Test:* Read each paragraph aloud and mark where you feel a "click" — a sudden shift between narrator and character. *Fix:* Apply the entry and exit patterns. If the click is on entry, use a sensory anchor or perception verb fade. If on exit, use detail escalation or a hard cut.

**Confusion with stream of consciousness** — *Test:* Does the passage maintain third-person grammar? If yes, it's free indirect. If grammar dissolves, it's stream of consciousness. Different tools.

## Source
James Wood, *How Fiction Works* (2008). Traces the technique from Austen (ironic free indirect — the narrator quietly mocking the character's self-image) through Flaubert (lyrical — the narrator dissolving into sensory world) to Woolf (the entire narrative surface — no stable narrator remains).
