# Objective Correlative

## One-liner
Externalize internal emotion through a concrete object, scene, or chain of events — make the reader feel without telling them what to feel.

## The Problem
"She was devastated." The reader processes this as information, not experience. Naming an emotion activates the brain's semantic network ("I know what devastation means") but not its simulation network. A word like "devastated" is a *label for* an emotion. A correlative is a *cause of* one.

## Trigger Patterns
Grep your draft for these. Each hit is a candidate for conversion.

| Pattern | Regex | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Emotion-naming verbs | `(felt\|realized\|knew\|understood\|sensed)\s+(a \|the )?(sadness\|grief\|rage\|joy\|dread\|guilt\|shame\|longing\|relief)` | "She felt a deep sadness" |
| State-of-being emotion | `was\s+(devastated\|overcome\|filled with\|seized by\|consumed by\|paralyzed by\|overwhelmed)` | "He was overcome with guilt" |
| Interiority stage directions | `a\s+(wave\|pang\|surge\|rush\|stab\|jolt\|flood)\s+of` | "A wave of grief hit her" |
| Redundant framing after image | Any sentence after a concrete detail that explains what it means | "She picked up his glasses *and felt a wave of grief*" |
| Adverb-driven emotion | `(smiled\|stared\|laughed\|whispered\|walked)\s+(sadly\|blankly\|bitterly\|angrily\|numbly\|wearily)` | "She smiled sadly" |

Not every hit needs conversion. Use correlatives for moments that must land emotionally. Exposition and transitions can name emotions without damage.

## Procedure

When you catch a telling sentence:

1. **DIAGNOSE the exact emotion.**
   Not "sad" — what KIND? Absence-grief? Regret-grief? Guilt-sadness? The correlative can only be as precise as your diagnosis. "She was sad" — she's sad because the person is gone and she didn't say what she needed to. That's regret-grief, not generic sadness.

2. **ASK: what would a camera see?**
   You can't film "she was sad." What is she doing, touching, looking at? What in the room has changed or hasn't changed?

3. **FIND the object that PRODUCES that emotion.**
   Not symbolizes it — produces it. Three requirements:
   - Specific to this character and situation (not a generic photo or ring — unless a particular detail makes it unrepeatable)
   - Would affect someone who'd never read a novel
   - Could plausibly exist in the world of the story without the author placing it there

4. **SELECT the correlative type based on what the emotion does.**

   | The emotion is about... | Use | Why |
   |---|---|---|
   | Something or someone missing | **Object** — show what the absent person left behind | Presence of the object makes the absence concrete |
   | Internal conflict or indecision | **Action** — show contradictory behavior | The body reveals what the mind won't say |
   | A place that has changed in meaning | **Environment** — show the space as altered | Setting becomes emotional evidence |
   | Shutdown, numbness, dissociation | **Sensory mismatch** — show a gap between stimulus and response | The gap IS the emotional state |
   | Something too complex for one object | **Accumulation** — layer 3+ neutral objects at different timescales | They triangulate a shape the text never names |

5. **EMBED the object. DELETE the telling sentence.**
   No framing sentences before or after. Drop it in cold. Don't hedge. Don't add "and somehow that made it worse." The object is the sentence now.

6. **READ IT BACK COLD.**
   Three tests:
   - You feel the emotion without the deleted sentence → it works.
   - You feel the *writer* → the object is too poetic. Return to step 3 and reach for something more ordinary.
   - You feel nothing → the object isn't charged enough. Return to step 3.

## Techniques

**1. Object as emotion**
Bad: "She was grieving."
Good: "His reading glasses were still on the nightstand. She picked them up. The lenses still had his fingerprints."
Why: fingerprints imply a living body — on a dead person's object, the reader's mind bridges the gap, and the bridge is grief.

**2. Action as emotion**
Bad: "He was lonely."
Good: "He set two plates for dinner. Paused. Put one back. Then took it out again."
Why: the indecision reveals he knows the truth but can't inhabit it yet.

**3. Environment as emotion**
Bad: "The house felt threatening."
Good: "Every clock in the house had stopped at 3:47."
Why: one synchronized wrong detail implies an agent or event you can't see, and dread is the imagination filling in what could cause this.

**4. Sensory mismatch as emotion**
Bad: "She felt numb after the news."
Good: "The kettle was screaming. She watched it. After a while she made tea. It was already cold when she noticed it in her hand."
Why: the gap between stimulus and response is dissociation made visible — time passes and she doesn't register it, so neither does the reader.

**5. Accumulated correlatives**
For emotions too complex for a single object, layer multiple objects that are individually neutral but collectively devastating.
"The dog kept going to the door. The mail was piling up. Someone had left flowers on the step, and they were beginning to turn."
Why: each object implies a different duration of absence — hours, days, a week — and together they triangulate a shape the text never names.

## Calibration

| Failure mode | Symptom | Diagnostic question | Fix |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Too subtle** | Object is real but uncharged | Could this object appear in any scene without changing tone? | Add the specific detail that collides with the situation. Not the glasses — the fingerprints on the glasses. |
| **Too conspicuous** | Reader sees the writer, not the scene | Could this detail plausibly exist without the author placing it? | Replace the striking image with an ordinary object in extraordinary context. |
| **Dead correlative** | Rain for sadness, storms for anger | Has the reader encountered this object in this emotional role before? | Find an object specific enough that it hasn't been cast in this part yet. |
| **Stacking without variation** | Five objects all pointing the same way feel like a list | Do the objects operate at different scales or timelines? | Vary by duration, distance, or sensory channel (the dog=hours, mail=days, flowers=a week). |

**The calibration rule**: the strongest correlatives are ordinary objects in extraordinary context. The charge comes from the collision between object and situation, not from the object alone. If you're reaching for a striking image, you've left correlative territory and entered symbolism.

## Common Pitfalls

- **Symbols instead of correlatives**: A dove doesn't *produce* peace — it *references* peace through cultural convention. Test: would this object produce the emotion in someone from a completely different culture? If it depends on learned association, it's a symbol. If it depends on human experience of the physical world, it's a correlative.
- **Over-explaining the object**: "She picked up his glasses and felt a wave of grief." Every word after "glasses" is the writer flinching. Delete from the emotion-word backward until only the object remains.
- **Overcorrection into craft**: After learning this skill, writers produce correlatives that are *too* good — poetic, striking, perfect. The best correlatives are plain objects that become devastating only because of what the reader already knows about the scene. Reach for the ordinary.
